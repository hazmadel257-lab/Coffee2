Beans

  • Ethiopian coffee

Coffee production in Ethiopia is an ancient tradition dating back centuries. Ethiopia accounts for approximately 3% of the global coffee market.Coffee is vital to the Ethiopian economy; about 60% of the country's foreign income comes from coffee, and an estimated 15 million people depend on some aspect of coffee production for their livelihoods. In 2006, coffee exports brought in $350 million, representing 34% of total exports that year

  • Colombian coffee

Colombian coffee is a type of coffee bean produced in Colombia and is considered a favorite among coffee lovers worldwide due to its unique and distinctive flavor. Colombian coffee beans are characterized by their excellent balance of acidity and aroma.It typically features medium to high acidity and a sweet, balanced flavor. Colombian coffee has a rich body and distinctive aroma that sets it apart from other coffee varieties Colombian coffee is considered a premium variety in the coffee world, enjoying an excellent reputation for quality and excellence. Colombian coffee beans are used to prepare a wide variety of coffee drinks, such as espresso, filter coffee, and cold brew, making them highly sought after by both coffee enthusiasts and professionals in the coffee industry.

  • Brazilian coffee

Brazilian coffee is a type of coffee bean produced in Brazil, one of the world's largest coffee-producing countries. Brazilian coffee has a distinctive and unique flavor, characterized by its full body and rich aroma.- This makes it appealing to coffee lovers from diverse cultures and nationalities. The quality of Brazilian coffee depends on several factors, such as soil type, climate in the growing regions, and processing techniques, resulting in a variety and richness of flavors that reflect Brazil's coffee heritage. Brazilian coffee beans are used to prepare a wide variety of coffee drinks, from espresso and filter coffee to cold brew and specialty coffees. Brazilian coffee is a favorite among many baristas and coffee connoisseurs due to its smooth body and deep flavor, which enhances the coffee experience significantly.

  • Kenyan coffee

Coffee production is a significant contributor to the economy of Kenya.[1] The industry is noted for its cooperative system of production, processing, milling, marketing, and auction system. About 70% of Kenyan coffee is produced in small farms that control about 75% of the land under production.[2][3] It was estimated in 2012 that there were about 150,000 coffee farmers in Kenya.[4] Other sources suggest that 6 million Kenyans are employed directly or indirectly in the coffee industry.[5] The acidic soil in the highlands of central Kenya together with just the right amount of sunlight and rainfall provide excellent conditions for growing coffee plants. However, due to a property boom in areas that grow coffee and price instability,[6][4] production in this African Great Lakes country fell from about 130,000 tonnes in 1987/8 to 40,000 tonnes in 2011/12.[7] Kenya largely produces Arabica blend coffee.[8] Coffee from Kenya is of the 'Colombia mild' type, and is well known for its intense flavor, full body, and pleasant aroma with notes of cocoa, high grade coffee from Kenya is one of the most sought-after coffees in the world.[4]

  • Jamaican coffee

Jamaican Blue Mountain Coffee or Jamaica Blue Mountain Coffee is a classification of coffee grown in the Blue Mountains of Jamaica. The coffee was introduced to Jamaica in 1728.[1] Jamaican Blue Mountain Coffee is an Arabica coffee-bean variety called Typica,[2] which originated in southwestern Ethiopia.[3] The best lots of Blue Mountain coffee are noted for their mild flavour and lack of bitterness. Over the past few decades, this coffee has developed a reputation that has made it one of the world's most expensive and sought-after coffees. Over 80% of all Jamaican Blue Mountain Coffee is exported to Japan.[4] In addition to their use for brewed coffee, the beans are the flavour base of Tia Maria coffee liqueur. Jamaican Blue Mountain Coffee is a globally protected certification mark, meaning only coffee certified by the Jamaica Commodities Regulatory Authority[5] (previously the Coffee Industry Board of Jamaica) can be labelled as such. It comes from a recognised growing region in the Blue Mountain region of Jamaica, and the Coffee Industry Board of Jamaica monitors its cultivation. Generally located between Kingston to the south and Port Antonio to the north, the Blue Mountains are some of the highest mountains in the Caribbean, rising to 2,256 metres (7,402 ft). The region's climate is cool and misty with high rainfall, and the soil is rich with excellent drainage, conditions considered ideal for coffee cultivation
